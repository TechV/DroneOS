Build

The original design during development under went a few changes as I began to start considering better spacing of the components. To keep the design modular, much of the parts are positioned using industrial strength 20lb velcro. The Pi which acts as the brain of our drone sits at the very top of the drone. Next to the Pi is the Alfa wifi module. Under these components is our battery which rests on top of a plastic plate (used for cable management purposes). Under this is the top plate of the frame. Between the two plates of the frame is where I stowed away the breadboard I put the accelerometer/gyro breakout board onto by use of the t-cobbler breakout. This keeps the accelerometer/gyro stable and protected. Finally, on the underside of the bottom plate I have mounted the Qbrain ESC controller.

Power

Power is distributed from the battery via two points. The first is via the balance connector on the battery. This cable plugs directly into the FPV transmitter for the direct streaming camera included with the FatShark goggles. The other power connection is the red and black leads which are connected directly to the power input on the Qbrain. Since the Qbrain includes a 5V output we can use that to power our Pi. If you choose a different ESC with no SBEC/UBEC 5V out feature, be sure to grab the 5V UBEC labeled optional in the parts list.

Control

This project aims to be open hardware and open software. There have been one or two simillar projects in the last year or two that have had some success in the realm of software based control. The most notable was published in The MagPi issue 19 & 20 by Andy Baker. The wiring I used was designed to align with wiring used in that project since the few other projects seem to base their wireing in the same way. By having a pre-selected wiring orientation we will ensure that software compatibility and options will be more readily available or developeable.

Beginning your build

When you first recieve your parts, you will quickly notice that we are are building this from scratch. The first step you want to start with is the frame. You will notice that the frame comes completly disassembled with no printed instruction manual. The manual is however available online as a pdf (or in this documentation below)

Frame Manual

Building the frame

The frame build is relatively straight forward using the pictures included in the manual as a reference. To the right you can see the pictures from my build. Notice that I did not use the four white plastic mounting screws in my build since I used a plastic plate to create a cavity to stash the slack from the wires and the FatShark transmitter. These mounting screws probably could be used to create a more reliable raised deck to store wires under if you wanted to go that route.

Proceed to motors

Installing the motors, props and ESC

Installation of the motors is rather straight forward. The arms of the frame have mounting holes that align with the mounting holes on our motors. Make sure the wires from the motor go off to the side to prevent damage from the bolt on the arm. I chose to mount my ESC centered on the bottom of my drone. I also braided the wires of the ESC to keep things neat and reduce any interference.
When connecting the motor leads to the ESC leads, it does'nt matter which wire goes with which color. The ESC automatically handles controlling the signals. Furthermore, when it comes time to change the spin dirrection of the motors, all you need to do is disconnect two of the wires and flip them and the ESC automatically changes the rotation.

Programming the ESC

We need to change a few settings on our esc before we begin wiring everything together. These options may not be entirely neccessary, but they should help us ensure proper performance. The programer card includes instructions and a connection cable to hook up the various leads for the motors. Pragramming needs to take place before wiring because we will be changing the pin order of the cables and the programmer will only work with the stock pinouts. In order to power the ESC and programing card you will need to attach one of your batteries to the ESC power inputs. You will see that during my build process I used solderless aligator clips to make these connections. Use the images to the right to guide you to set the ESC to the settings that should offer us the best responsiveness from our motors.

In order for our hardware to align with the current software that is available for the drone, it is important that we know what our orientation will be. To determine this, fold the frame and mount the ESC on the bottom as shown using 20lb velcro stickers. You can then braid the leads and connect them to the battery.

Proceed to Breadboard

Building the Breadboard

This is arguably the most complicated portion of the build process. It requires soldering skills and patience. Luckily, I've figgured out most of the hard work for you such as proper pinouts, the best way to manage the space and the best way to make the connections.

The first step you will need to do is assemble the T-cobler component and solder the pins in. I used a breadboard to get things organized so I soldered the pins onto the MPU6050 breakout board and the T-cobler board then removed the black spacer so the components lay flush on the breadboard.

If you have never used a breadboard before, it is essentially just an easy tool to aid in prototyping when you need to make connections temporarily or want to easily be able to change connections, and layouts or swap components out.

Wiring the Breadboard

The breadboard consists of two types of sonnections; the connections for the MPU6050, and the signal leads for the motors. What makes things complicated is the fact that there are multiple different types of breakout board for the MPU6050. Some of these boards have different labeling or pins. The specific breakout board I used is the one available from sparkfun.

When it comes to choosing how to wire things it is important to note that different controller software options have different default pin mappings. Typically these mappings are easy to change. The main input for the MPU6050 is the i2c bus, which is the scl and sda pins on the T-cobler.

It is important to test all of your connections to help avoid any issues that may arrise. Initial testing can be done with a circuit tester and final testing can be done with some software scripts. demo_3d will show that the MPU6050 is working by generating a rotatable 3d model. My motor test script will then allow you to test your motors. It is important to note that different software will have the motors on different gpio pin configurations. Also, things with the pi seem to act strangely at times depending on distro. If you encounter a state where one of your gpio outputs no longer responds try moving one of the motors to a different gpio port.

Proceed to software

Software Control

Since the goal of this project is open hardware and software we have a couple different selections of controller software that is capable of running our hardware. It is the job of the software to take readings from the MPU6050 and convert them into instructions for the power levels on each of the motors. Since this is the first project with the goal of creating a unified hardware platform for a rasberry pi powered drone, we do currently run into some issues with different pins and methods of servo control accross these various software controllers. Luckily, all of these options are open source and allow you to hardcode in pin options.

Pistuffings Quadcopter

This software comes from a developer named Andy Baker who was featured in The MagPi magazine for his Raspberry Pi drone. This controller is python based and is currently focused on automated flight and manual configuration of components. Also pinouts need to be changed to fit hardware.

Picopter

This controller is the one currently in use. The original developer abandoned the fully pi controlled method in favor of avr controll. I currently maintain a fork of the old controller and have been experimenting with adapting it to overcome the limitations of the pi.

Others

In my research I have come accross various other controller projects, in various states. However they lacked proper documentation or relied heavily on extra hardware to control the motors. The goal is to use as little external hardware as possible.
